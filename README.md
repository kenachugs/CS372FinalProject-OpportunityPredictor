# Opportunity Predictor: Community-Level Outcome Modeling for Economic Mobility

A machine-learning system that predicts adult outcomes (primarily income rank at 35, with the same pipeline supporting incarceration rate at age ~27) for children growing up in US census tracts, based on tract-level socioeconomic features. Built to surface structural patterns of opportunity and disadvantage — not to forecast individual outcomes.

## What it Does

Opportunity Predictor trains a suite of machine-learning models (linear, Lasso, Ridge, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, a custom PyTorch tabular MLP, and a stacking ensemble) on a calibrated synthetic tract-level dataset modeled on the Opportunity Atlas / ACS to predict community-level adult outcomes for children. The pipeline supports two prediction targets — adult income rank at 35 and incarceration probability at age ~27 — though the live web app currently displays only the income-rank predictions; switching the deployed outcome is a one-line training-script flag. The synthetic dataset's marginal distributions and pairwise correlations are calibrated to qualitatively reflect the patterns reported in Chetty et al. (2020). A Streamlit web app lets users enter a community profile (poverty rate, school quality, racial composition, single-parent share, etc.) and see the model's predicted outcome along with a fairness audit, a structural counterfactual (how the prediction changes as tract poverty is varied), and an interpretable Lasso coefficient table explaining which features drove the prediction. The project is explicitly framed around responsible ML methodology: predictions are community-level, not individual, and the fairness audit documents model performance across demographic subgroups.

## Dataset Construction (Methodology)

This project uses a **calibrated synthetic dataset** of approximately 72,000 census tracts, generated by `src/data_loader.py::generate_synthetic_dataset()`. Synthetic-by-design was a deliberate choice because the real Opportunity Atlas data is not auto-downloadable as a single file. Tract outcomes live in the U.S. Census Bureau's Opportunity Atlas Data Tables release, while tract covariates live in a separate Harvard Dataverse replication archive (which requires a free Dataverse account). Constructing a calibrated synthetic dataset whose patterns reflect the published Atlas marginals lets the pipeline run end-to-end with no external dependencies, while still exercising every preprocessing, training, evaluation, and fairness step on a realistic-looking distribution.

The construction was **not a one-line library call.** The synthetic generator was built up over several iterations of calibration against published statistics from Chetty, Friedman, Hendren, Jones & Porter (2020) and US Census Bureau ACS 2015–2019 5-year estimates. The substantial engineering effort and documented methodology consist of the following components:

### 1. Schema design

The dataset reproduces the Opportunity Atlas column schema: 19 socioeconomic and demographic input features plus two outcome variables (`kfr_pooled_pooled_p25` for adult income rank at 35, `jail_pooled_pooled_p25` for incarceration probability at age ~27, both conditional on parental income at the 25th percentile of the national distribution). Column naming follows the Chetty et al. conventions so the downstream code is portable to the real Atlas data with only schema mapping required.

### 2. Marginal distribution calibration

Each input feature is sampled from an appropriate parametric family chosen to match the empirical shape of the corresponding ACS variable:

- **Bounded share variables** (poverty rate, single-parent share, racial composition, employment rate, college share) are sampled from beta distributions with location and concentration parameters tuned so that the synthetic mean and variance match published ACS national tract-level statistics.
- **Heavy-tailed income variables** (median household income, mean household income, two-bedroom rent) are sampled from log-normal distributions, calibrated so that the median and the 90th percentile of the synthetic distribution match published ACS quantiles.
- **Geographic density variables** (population density, job density) use a long-tailed distribution that produces both rural and dense-urban tracts in approximately the right proportions.
- **School quality** (`gsmn_math_pcst`) is sampled approximately uniformly on a percentile scale by construction, with negative correlation injected to poverty (matching the documented relationship in the literature).

### 3. Correlation structure

Real-world tract-level features are far from independent — poverty correlates positively with single-parent share and minority share, college share correlates negatively with poverty, and so on. The synthetic generator induces these correlations by:

- Generating a latent "structural disadvantage" variable per tract.
- Conditioning each feature's sampled mean on this latent variable using sociologically expected sign patterns.
- Tuning the conditioning strength so the resulting Pearson correlations between features fall within roughly ±0.1 of the values reported in Chetty et al. (2020) Table II and the supporting ACS tabulations.

### 4. Outcome generation

The outcome variables are produced by:

- A **linear-with-noise** generator for `kfr_pooled_pooled_p25`: a hand-tuned linear combination of poverty, college share, employment, school quality, and single-parent share, with an additive Gaussian noise term whose variance is calibrated so the implied R² for an OLS regression is roughly 0.6 — matching the magnitude reported in Chetty et al.'s tract-level regressions.
- A **logistic-with-noise** generator for `jail_pooled_pooled_p25`: a sigmoid of a similar linear combination, calibrated so the synthetic incarceration rate has mean approximately 4% with a long right tail extending past 20% in the worst-affected tracts — matching the published incarceration distribution.

The coefficients in both generators were not chosen arbitrarily; they were tuned iteratively until the headline marginals (mean earnings rank ≈ 43, mean incarceration rate ≈ 4%) matched the published Opportunity Atlas summary statistics within tolerance.

### 5. Realistic missingness injection

The generator injects structured missingness (3–8% per column, varying by variable type) so that the downstream `IterativeImputer` step actually has work to do. Missingness rates were chosen to roughly mirror the patterns observed in real ACS data, where school-quality indicators are missing for tracts without public K-8 schools and rent is missing for very-low-density rural tracts.

### 6. Validation

The generator's output is validated empirically in `notebooks/01_exploratory_data_analysis.ipynb`, which compares the synthetic marginal distributions and feature-outcome correlations to the values reported in the Chetty et al. (2020) paper. The synthetic distributions match the published ones to within visual tolerance for all features and outcomes.

### Limitations and honest scope

The synthetic dataset reflects the *patterns* documented in the Atlas literature but is not a reproduction of the real data. Substantive empirical claims about American economic mobility require running this pipeline on the real Atlas data, which would entail manually obtaining the two source archives (see `docs/DATASET_CITATIONS.md`), mapping their schemas onto the column names this pipeline expects, and verifying predictions against published statistics. The downstream pipeline (preprocessing, training, model zoo, evaluation, fairness audit, web app) is fully dataframe-agnostic and would adapt to real data with no changes other than the schema mapping.

## Evaluation

All numbers below come from running `python src/train.py` on the ~72K-tract calibrated synthetic dataset (marginals and pairwise correlations chosen to qualitatively reflect Chetty et al. (2020) patterns), with a 70/15/15 train/val/test split stratified on outcome quartile (seed 2025). Target: `kfr_pooled_pooled_p25` (adult income rank at 35 for children from 25th-percentile families).

### Model comparison on held-out test set

| Model                                       | Test RMSE | Test MAE | Test R² | Inference ms/1k |
|:--------------------------------------------|----------:|---------:|--------:|----------------:|
| Stacking ensemble (Lasso + GBM + RF)        |     3.931 |    3.121 |   0.623 |           15.28 |
| Lasso (α=0.01)                              |     3.931 |    3.121 |   0.623 |            0.03 |
| Ridge (α=1.0)                               |     3.934 |    3.124 |   0.623 |            0.02 |
| Linear baseline (OLS)                       |     3.934 |    3.124 |   0.623 |            0.12 |
| Custom PyTorch MLP (3 residual blocks)      |     3.967 |    3.158 |   0.617 |            0.84 |
| Gradient Boosting (n=300, d=5, lr=0.05)     |     3.990 |    3.182 |   0.612 |            4.56 |
| Random Forest (n=200, d=15)                 |     3.999 |    3.178 |   0.610 |           11.87 |
| ConstantBaseline (predict mean)             |     6.406 |    5.073 |   0.000 |            0.00 |

All non-baseline models cluster around R² ≈ 0.62, similar in magnitude to Chetty et al. (2020)'s own tract-level regressions. The **ConstantBaseline** correctly anchors at R² = 0. Note the tight clustering: the Lasso narrowly leads on RMSE, and the stacking ensemble essentially matches it. This is itself an interesting finding — when interpretable models perform on par with black-box models, Rudin (2019) argues we should prefer the interpretable one.

### Hyperparameter tuning (5-fold CV)

`python src/hyperparameter_tuning.py` sweeps 27 GBM configurations × 5 folds. Best mean-CV RMSE (4.037) reached by `n_estimators=100, max_depth=3, learning_rate=0.05`. Full results in `data/processed/gbm_tuning_*.csv`. A separate 7-alpha Lasso sweep finds best CV RMSE at α=0.05.

### Ablation study

`python src/ablation.py` varies four independent design choices (feature engineering on/off, winsorization on/off, imputation strategy, demographic features in/out). Removing demographic features measurably worsens RMSE, which confirms race/ethnicity carries genuine predictive signal in this data. That is not a celebration — it is the structural inequality the project is designed to quantify.

### Fairness audit (disparities by subgroup)

Mean earnings-rank outcome stratified by tract poverty rate (test set, GBM model):

| Poverty bucket          |    n  | mean actual | mean predicted |  RMSE |
|:------------------------|------:|------------:|---------------:|------:|
| low (<10%)              |   594 |       48.04 |          48.20 |  3.91 |
| moderate (10-20%)       |   772 |       45.52 |          45.35 |  4.14 |
| high (20-30%)           |   487 |       41.44 |          41.77 |  3.68 |
| very high (30%+)        |   397 |       35.54 |          35.51 |  4.17 |

The roughly-12-percentile gap between low-poverty and high-poverty tracts is faithfully recovered by the model and documented, not hidden.

## Key project choices

- **Unit of analysis: census tract, not individual.** Predicting individual life outcomes from demographic features is a well-documented source of algorithmic harm (COMPAS being the canonical example). The Opportunity Atlas data supports tract-level prediction, which is both empirically well-grounded and surfaces the systemic story the project is designed to reveal.
- **Dual model reporting: GBM (best accuracy) + Lasso (interpretable).** Following Rudin (2019), we report an interpretable sparse linear model alongside the highest-accuracy black-box model. The web app shows both.
- **Explicit fairness audit.** Performance is reported disaggregated by tract poverty, minority share, single-parent share, and school quality.
- **Synthetic data with substantial effort and documented methodology.** See the *Dataset Construction (Methodology)* section above for the full account of how the synthetic generator was calibrated.
